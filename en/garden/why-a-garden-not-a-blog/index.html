Why a garden, not a blog

I've kept a blog. I've abandoned a blog. I've come back. I've left again. The loop wore me out: each post started with the feeling of needing to say something round, finished, something that earned the "Publish" button.

A garden doesn't ask any of that. A note can be a question. A list. A quote with an awkward comment beside it. The only thing it asks is that you come back to water it.

The metaphor isn't decoration. A blog is a conveyor belt — you write, you publish, the next thing pushes the last thing off the screen. A garden is topology: each note has a place, linked to others, reached by affinity more than by date. An idea from two years ago can sit beside one from last week if they're in conversation.

It also changes what you write. On the blog I tend to close too early, to force a conclusion. In the garden I let myself leave the sentence half-done and come back tomorrow. That's what's surprised me most: how much the content shifts when the container shifts.

It's not for everyone. If what you love is the ritual of publication, the blog has its own poetry. But if what you're hauling around is notebooks full of ideas you never quite polish, a garden gives you permission to let them grow in public.
